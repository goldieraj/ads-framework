# Hermes Image Ad Framework

The image ad landscape is currently dominated by **"Ugly Ads"** and **Long-Form Advertorial Copy**. Based on an analysis of over 25,000 image ads across the health, gadget, and pain relief niches, the highest-performing image ads do not look like traditional marketing. They use low-production visuals as a "pattern interrupt" to stop the scroll, relying almost entirely on the primary text (often 1,500+ words) to do the selling.

## The 4 Pillars of High-Converting Image Ads

### 1. The "Ugly Ad" Visual Pattern Interrupt
The most successful image ads intentionally avoid polished, studio-quality product photography. They use visuals that look native to a user's organic feed or look like clinical documentation.

* **Medical/Clinical Imagery:** X-rays, anatomical illustrations (e.g., prostate, knee joints), EKG monitors. These stop the scroll because they imply a serious health conversation, not a product pitch.
* **Low-Production Personal Photos:** Casual photos of older adults (e.g., Dr. Ruth White on a beach) or close-ups of body parts (e.g., arthritic hands). These serve as "trust signals" that a real person is speaking.
* **Graphic/Shock Imagery:** Surgery scars or wounds. These are highly disruptive pattern interrupts that trigger immediate curiosity and fear.
* **The "Hero Ingredient" Shot:** Showing raw ingredients (e.g., fresh berberine berries) instead of a manufactured supplement bottle.
* **Zero Text Overlays:** Unlike traditional best practices, the top-performing ads in these niches use **no text overlays** on the images. The image is purely visual bait.

### 2. The Long-Form "Advertorial" Primary Text
The image stops the scroll; the primary text does the selling. Top-performing ads use the primary text field as a full landing page/advertorial, often exceeding 1,500 words.

* **The Authority Hook:** "I've been a physical therapist for sixteen years. And I have a confession..."
* **The Specific Story Hook:** "I found a wallet on the dining room table on a Wednesday night... It was Frank's."
* **The Villain:** Identifying an enemy to rally against. Examples include $400 Apple Watches, $12 drugstore knee braces, prescription Gabapentin, or the threat of surgery.
* **The Mechanism Explanation:** Using simple analogies to explain complex biology (e.g., "Think about it like a houseplant. If you forget to water it for months...").
* **The Soft Close:** "Here's the link if you want to learn more. No pressure."

### 3. The Curiosity-Driven Link Headline
The link headline (the bold text under the image) is almost universally framed as a question or a shocking statement, rather than a functional product description.

* **Question Framing:** "Why Are You Paying $400 Just to See Your Heart Rate?" or "What Is Waiting Really Costing Your Knees?"
* **Authority Endorsement:** "Orthopedic surgeon: 'Why I stopped recommending shoulder surgery'"
* **Curiosity Gap:** "Wait.. You Tell Your Patients to do What?"

### 4. The Link Description Social Proof
The small text below the link headline is used exclusively for social proof or urgent benefits.

* **Volume Proof:** "100,000+ Sold"
* **Testimonial Snippet:** "'This actually worked, thank you so much.' - Emily T."
* **Specific Benefit:** "See your heart rate, BP, and oxygen now."

## Persona & Angle Testing Strategy

High-volume advertisers (like RejuvaCare with 720+ active ads) do not test just one angle. They test the same product through multiple distinct "Personas," each with their own dedicated Facebook Page.

| Persona Type | Angle Focus | Example Hook |
|---|---|---|
| **The Insider Expert** | "What my industry gets wrong" | "I've performed over 3,000 rotator cuff surgeries... and I was wrong." |
| **The Frustrated Patient** | "The system failed me until I found this" | "Three years ago, I had rotator cuff surgery. Last week, my surgeon told me I need another one." |
| **The Caring Spouse/Family** | "I watched my loved one suffer" | "My brother Dale drove 4 hours through a January blizzard to ask me a question..." |
| **The Medical Educator** | "The science behind the symptom" | "If your hands go numb when you're sleeping... your nerve is sending a final warning." |

## Format-Specific Rules & Execution

### For Gadgets & Devices (e.g., Knee Sleeves, Red Light Therapy)
* **Visual:** Product-in-use photography in a real-world setting (e.g., a knee sleeve on a leg walking down wooden stairs), or a split-screen before/after X-ray.
* **Copy:** Focus on the mechanical failure of the joint and how the device stabilizes or feeds the tissue. Attack cheap alternatives.

### For Supplements (e.g., Turmeric, Liver Support)
* **Visual:** Raw ingredients, personal photos of the "doctor/founder," or abstract pattern interrupts (e.g., a toilet bowl for a liver detox ad).
* **Copy:** Focus on cellular-level mechanisms (e.g., inflammation, absorption rates). Attack grocery store alternatives for low potency.

### Creative Scaling Protocol
When you find a winning image/copy combination, scale it by iterating on the **Hook** and the **Persona** while keeping the core mechanism and image the same. (e.g., RejuvaCare running 14 ads with the exact same image but testing the PT persona vs. the Surgeon persona).
