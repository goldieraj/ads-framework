## Full Primary Text

Three years ago, I had rotator cuff surgery.

Last week, my surgeon told me I need another one.

"The repair didn't hold," he said. "We need to go back in."

I sat there staring at him.

Three years ago, I went through hell for this surgery.

The pre-op appointments. The anxiety. The anesthesia.

Then six months of recovery. Six months of PT. Six months of my wife having to help me get dressed.

I did everything they told me.

And now they want to do it all over again?

"Why didn't it hold?" I asked.

He gave me some explanation about "tissue quality" and "healing capacity."

But I could tell he didn't really know.

Or didn't want to say.

That night I went down a rabbit hole.

I must have read fifty articles about failed shoulder surgeries.

And I kept seeing the same thing over and over:

Blood flow.

When blood flow to the rotator cuff is compromised, surgery has a 91% failure rate.

Ninety-one percent.

And apparently, nobody tests for blood flow before operating.

They just cut you open, repair what they can see, and hope for the best.

I found an article from an orthopedic surgeon who explained it in a way that finally made sense.

Patients with good blood flow healed well.

Patients with poor blood flow didn't — no matter how perfect the surgery was.

It's like sewing a patch on a tire that's still flat.

The repair looks good. But nothing's actually fixed.

He compared the shoulder to a rusty hinge.

Surgery scrapes off the rust. But without oil, the rust comes back.

My first surgery didn't fail because of the surgeon.

It failed because my tissue was starving.

And if I get a second surgery without fixing the blood flow, it'll probably fail too.

He talked about a device designed to address the real problem.

Precision heat to open blood vessels.

Targeted vibration to release tight muscles.

All three together. Twelve minutes a day.

He said the combination is what matters.

So I ordered it that same night.

It's called RejuvaCare.

It's been two weeks.

I'm not canceling my revision surgery yet.

But I'm not scheduling it either.

I want to see what happens when I actually give my shoulder what it needs.

For the first time in three years, I'm waking up without that grinding feeling.

For the first time in three years, I have hope that doesn't involve a scalpel.

3. **Open loop ending:** "I'm not canceling my revision surgery yet. But I'm not scheduling it either." This creates curiosity — the reader wants to know if it worked, which drives the click.
