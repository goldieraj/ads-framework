### Core Philosophy
- "The order of the proof points is probably more important than most people realize"
- Short copy wins when the buyer's pain is already loud
- Animated problem visuals make uncomfortable offers easier to watch
- Rick & Morty / Pixar style ads shouldn't start with the product — start with the moment people recognize

### Frameworks Identified

#### 1. The Contamination Frame
- Works in private/shame health categories
- Three moves: Demote buyer's current belief → Escalate 3 consequences (mild to fatal) → Anchor fix to near-future authority
- Ports to: bloating, bad breath, snoring, foot odor, vaginal dryness, ED, hemorrhoids

#### 2. The Brevity Strategy (Hearing Aids)
- "Say goodbye to expensive hearing aids 👇" — 6 words + emoji
- Works because: older buyer, price-anger angle, no product name to Google, only path is the link
- Video creative does the heavy lifting the copy refuses to

#### 3. The Shared Frustration Funnel
- Product isn't introduced until trust is earned through pain recognition
- "Graveyard of half-used jars under the sink" — name what every jar got wrong
- Itemized confession for 600+ words before mentioning product
- Each line is a receipt the reader recognizes from their own drawer

#### 4. The Escalation Ladder
- Common problem → Serious consequence → Existential threat
- Each escalation widens perceived stakes by an order of magnitude
- Triggers disgust response which overrides price sensitivity

#### 5. AI Animation Strategy (IM8 Health - $100M ARR in 11 months)
- AI Animations allow leveraging AI content without damaging brand
- Start with the moment people recognize (tired eyes, messy kitchen, open chip bags)
- The craving becomes the villain, the kitchen becomes the battlefield
- Character looks exactly like the customer feels

#### 6. The Nattokinase "Teach Before Sell" Pattern
- "Many brands advertise 8,000 FU on the front, but quietly split that dose across 3-4 capsules"
- Educate on industry deception → Position as the honest alternative
- Works because it creates a "now I can't unsee it" moment

#### 7. The $37 Neuropathy Solution Pattern
- Three things in twelve words: small price ($37), condition name (neuropathy), location (your living room)
- Filters audience automatically — only people with neuropathy click
- Kills objections about clinics, appointments, copays
- "The headline IS the offer"

### Tactical Patterns
1. **No product name in hook** — nothing to Google, nothing to bounce on
2. **Future date authority** — "Recommended in 2026" implies institutional update
3. **Private shame categories** — buyer won't Google deeply, uniquely vulnerable to single ad
4. **Itemized receipts** — specific dollar amounts ($3,400 over 4 years, $140 weighted blanket)
5. **Small time units** — "7-second fix", "10 minutes a day"
6. **Contamination language** — "toxic reabsorption" triggers primal disgust
7. **Belief demotion** — tell buyer what they currently believe, signal it's wrong

### 8. The "Frustrated Man in His Truck" Pattern (Joint Supplements)
A brand doing $1.7M/month selling joint support to men 45+ who still lift/golf/run. The breakthrough: a 54-year-old contractor sitting in his truck before work, ranting for 80 seconds about pain creams and cheap joint pills. Product doesn't appear until the final 20 seconds. That ad hit $9K/day in spend within 3 weeks. Key insight: "In markets where every brand sounds calm, polished, and science-backed, the brand willing to sound fed up feels more believable. Because the customer is already angry. You're just giving them the words." Testing cadence: 12-16 new angles per week.

### 9. The "Cornerstones" Category Creation Pattern (Aonic)
Instead of competing on "best vitamin," invent a new mental shelf. "Four cornerstones of supplementation" makes every basic multi look incomplete by comparison. Four is the right number — three feels like a starter pack, five feels like a kitchen sink. Stop selling SKUs, start selling shelves.

### 10. The Clinical Number Drop Pattern (Fleava Hair)
"In a 150-day clinical study comparing Fleava to a placebo, users saw a 98% increase in new hair growth." Statistical anchoring — once the reader sees 98, 96, and 100 in the first two sentences, every subsequent claim inherits credibility. The brain doesn't separate proven claims from adjacent claims when they share a paragraph.

### 11. Founder-Led Creative (Running 77 Days)
Founder ads are printing across niches. Real person, real problem, real solution. No flashy production. The "simplify your stack" angle has massive white space in anti-aging. 144 founder ads collected across different niches as reference.

### 12. The Dialogue Cold Open (Christian Devotional Niche)
"I told my husband I was walking away from Christianity. He said, 'No you're not. You're just done faking.'" Two real lines, first person, before the brand appears anywhere. Reads like a journal entry that wandered into the feed. 35 days active, performance score 91. The religion niche is brutal because most copy reads preachy — this one reads like confession.

### 13. Spanish Market Expansion
154 active ads in Mexico selling a validated USA supplement (Astaxanthin). Basic product page. Find the product, adapt to your market, print. Most founders see Spanish as a translation task — the best operators see it as a market.

### 14. Red Light Therapy Wavelength Specificity
"660nm red light. 850nm near-infrared." Those wavelengths sound clinical and make the product feel engineered. Real numbers in body copy outperform vague claims like "advanced technology" every time. The buyer doesn't need to understand the physics — they need to feel the brand respects them enough to share the receipt.

Claude turns one product into 5+ creative directions: mood angle, luxury angle, ingredient angle, evening routine angle, gift angle. Same product, different buyer desire, different visual frame, different reason to click. That's how you scale creative without inventing a new offer every week.
