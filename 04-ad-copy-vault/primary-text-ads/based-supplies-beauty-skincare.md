## PRODUCT 2: ONION HYDROSOL SHAMPOO

### ANGLE 4 — The Stylist Revelation (Long-Form Personal Story — Image Ads)

This is the **hero copy for image ads** (~67 image ads all running this same long-form story). It is a full personal story format — 1,500+ words — used as primary text on static image ads. This is exceptional: most brands use short copy for image ads. Based Supplies uses a complete presale-length story.

**Opening Hook:**
> Please STOP buying hair growth supplements until you check your shampoo.
>
> I know that sounds ridiculous. But I spent over $900 and nearly two years swallowing biotin pills, choking down collagen powder, and massaging serums into my scalp every night before bed.
>
> None of it grew back a single strand.
>
> And the reason none of it worked was something so obvious, so simple, so infuriating that when I finally figured it out, I sat on the edge of my bathtub and cried.
>
> The thing that was killing my hair was sitting on the shelf in my shower the entire time.

**The Villain Reveal (mid-story):**
> "They strip everything. That's their entire job. They're industrial-strength degreasers. The same chemical compound is in dish soap. Floor cleaner. Engine degreaser. And it's in about 90% of the shampoos on the shelf at Target."
>
> "Every time you wash your hair with a sulfate shampoo, it strips your scalp of its natural oils. That's why your hair feels 'squeaky clean' after. But it's also stripping something else. Something your follicles desperately need to survive."
>
> "Sulfur."

**The Mechanism:**
> "Sulfur is the building block of keratin. Keratin is literally what your hair is made of. Your scalp produces sulfur naturally, and your follicles need it to grow strong, thick hair."
>
> "But when you wash with sulfates every day — sometimes twice a day — you strip that sulfur away faster than your body can replace it."
>
> "Over months. Over years. Your follicles slowly starve. They weaken. They stop producing healthy hair. Eventually some of them go dormant entirely."

**The Suppression Claim:**
> The reason it's not mainstream in the West? Because there's no money in telling women to stop buying expensive supplements and fix their shampoo instead. The hair loss industry is worth billions. They need you buying pills, serums, treatments, subscription boxes, and 8-step "systems." They don't make money if the answer is "the problem is your shampoo, and a simple ingredient swap could change everything."

**The Specificity Proof:**
> Most shampoos that claim to contain "onion extract" or "onion oil" use almost none of it. A fraction of a percent. A trace amount. Just enough to put it on the label and charge a premium.
>
> It's like putting a single drop of orange juice in a glass of water and calling it "fresh-squeezed." Technically not a lie. Functionally useless.
>
> Fifteen percent. Not a splash. Not a marketing claim. Fifteen percent of the actual formula.

**The Week-by-Week Proof Sequence:**
> Week 1: Less hair in my brush. Not dramatic — but I noticed. Maybe 30% less.
> Week 2: Shedding continued to slow. My pillow was clean for the first time in months.
> Week 3: The brush was meeting more resistance — not from tangles, but from volume.
> Week 4: Baby hairs. Tiny little strands, maybe a quarter inch long, sticking up along my hairline.
> Week 6: My husband said "Is it just me, or does your hair look thicker?" (He doesn't notice anything.)
> Week 8: I wore my hair in a ponytail for the first time in over a year.

**The Close:**
> Stop stripping. Start feeding.
>
> Your hair is waiting to come back. You just have to stop standing in its way.
>
> P.S. — My biggest regret is the two years I wasted on supplements while the real problem was in my shower the whole time. Every single wash with sulfate shampoo was another day of stripping the sulfur my follicles needed. If someone had told me this 24 months ago, I would have saved $937 and more tears than I can count. Don't be me. Check your shampoo bottle tonight.

**Link Headline:** Get Your Thick Hair Back!
**Link Description:** LIMITED STOCK LEFT!

**Why this long-form image ad works:**
- The image creative (woman holding hair, before/after scalp) stops the scroll
- The long copy pre-qualifies: only the most interested readers get to the CTA
- The week-by-week proof sequence is a timeline credibility device — specific weeks = believable
- "$937 spent" is a precise number that signals authenticity (not "$1,000" or "hundreds")
- The P.S. is a standalone hook that works as a scroll-stopper for skimmers

**Second image ad copy angle (shorter):**

## IMAGE AD STYLES (67 image-only ads)

Based Supplies runs a distinct image creative strategy separate from video. All 67 image ads use the long-form "Stylist Revelation" personal story copy (Angle 4 above). The images themselves serve as scroll-stoppers while the copy does the selling.

### Image Style 1: Woman Pulling Hair Into Ponytail (Hero Image)
- **Subject:** Woman (30s–40s, relatable, not model-perfect) pulling her hair back into a ponytail, looking in mirror or at camera
- **Setting:** Bathroom, natural light
- **Mood:** Hopeful, confident — the "after" energy
- **Why it works:** The ponytail is the specific milestone mentioned in the copy ("I wore my hair in a ponytail for the first time in over a year"). Image and copy are locked together.

### Image Style 2: Product Jar on Clean Surface
- **Subject:** Tallow & Honey Balm jar, centered, minimal background
- **Setting:** White or natural wood surface, soft light
- **Mood:** Clean, premium, simple
- **Why it works:** Reinforces the "try almost nothing" simplicity claim — one jar, not a shelf of products

### Image Style 3: Before/After Scalp (Hair Parting)
- **Subject:** Close-up of hair parting — thin part on left, fuller part on right
- **Setting:** Overhead lighting to show scalp visibility
- **Mood:** Clinical proof
- **Why it works:** The "highway" part is the specific visual the copy describes. Seeing it confirms the story.

### Image Style 4: Product Lineup (Multi-Product)
- **Subject:** Multiple Based Supplies products arranged together (balm + shampoo + conditioner)
- **Setting:** Clean flat lay, natural background
- **Mood:** Brand credibility, range
- **Why it works:** Shows the brand has a full system, not just one product

### Image Style 5: Text-Overlay on Product Image
- **Subject:** Product jar with bold text overlay
- **Text examples seen:**
  - "Restore Your Skin in 3 Weeks"
  - "LIMITED STOCK LEFT"
  - "The All-in-One Skincare Solution"
- **Why it works:** Turns the image into a headline — works for skimmers who don't read copy

## VIDEO AD STYLES (1,000+ video ads)

### Video Style 1: UGC Talking Head — Bathroom Setting (Dominant)
- **Format:** Woman (30s–50s) speaking directly to camera in bathroom
- **Setting:** Bathroom counter visible, products in background or in hand
- **Hook types observed:**
  - Holding up product jar: "I need to tell you about this"
  - Showing scalp/hair: "Look at this — this is what my hair looked like"
  - Direct address: "If your skin looks like mine did six months ago..."
- **Duration:** 15s to 3:35 (same creator, multiple cut lengths)
- **Why it works:** Bathroom = natural context for skincare. Creator is relatable, not aspirational.

### Video Style 2: UGC Talking Head — Kitchen/Living Room Setting
- **Format:** Same talking head structure, different room
- **Setting:** Kitchen counter, living room couch
- **Why it works:** Different visual vehicle = different Entity ID from bathroom version, even with same copy

### Video Style 3: Product Demo — Application
- **Format:** Hands applying tallow balm to face or scalp
- **Setting:** Close-up, no face shown
- **Hook:** Shows texture, absorption, application technique
- **Why it works:** Demonstrates the "absorbs completely" claim visually

### Video Style 4: Before/After Transformation
- **Format:** Split screen or sequential clips showing hair/skin before and after
- **Duration:** 15–30s
- **Why it works:** Visual proof without requiring the viewer to read

### Video Style 5: Short Clip (11–27 seconds) — Hook Only
- **Format:** Single strong hook statement, product shown, CTA
- **Examples:** 0:11, 0:16, 0:24, 0:27 cuts visible in library
- **Why it works:** Tests the hook in isolation. If the hook works, extend to longer cut.

## SCALING STRATEGY OBSERVED

Based Supplies demonstrates the correct Entity ID scaling model:
1. **Lock the winning primary text** — Chemistry Lab copy unchanged for 13+ months
2. **Vary the visual vehicle** — same copy, different creator, different room, different cut length
3. **Run image ads simultaneously** — completely different format, different Entity ID, no overlap
4. **Use a second page (Bilxco)** — separate ad account for the villain angle, preventing overlap with the main page's exhaustion angle
5. **Test cut lengths** — 11s, 16s, 24s, 27s, 44s, 57s, 1:08, 1:13, 1:17, 1:26, 1:31, 1:35, 1:45, 1:58, 2:20, 2:25, 2:37, 2:39, 2:56, 3:34, 3:35 all active simultaneously
